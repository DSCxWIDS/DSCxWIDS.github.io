About the Event

Since its inception in 2020, the Data Science Club at NYU has been committed to providing opportunities for the student body. Our Datathon is one of our capstone events, bringing together hundreds of students to work with unique datasets and apply their skills to real world problems. This year, we have the honor of working with WiDS!


Challenges


In December 2017, the Urban Institute launched the Well-Being and Basic Needs Survey (WBNS), a nationally representative, internet-based survey of non-elderly adults designed to monitor changes in individual and family well-being during a time when policymakers are considering significant changes to federal safety net programs serving low-income families.

The 2020 round of the survey collects information on a broad array of topics related to health, material hardship, and the safety net, including health insurance, housing, food security, employment, family income, program participation, family financial security, disability, school enrollment, child care, COVID-19 vaccine attitudes, the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic on family employment, and immigration issues. It is available for your use on ICPSR.

Judging Criteria

The graduate students are looking to judge your solution in the following aspects:

  • Technical Skills:
    • Showing that you have engaged with the challenge and the data
    • An analysis of the data and any potential issues with it
    • Critical thinking about your work and findings
    • Being able to come up with something to present
  • Communication/Presentation Skills
    • Working as part of a team to produce a great presentation
    • Your ability to present to a non-technical audience
    • Your ability to be clear and concise
    • Creating figures that are suitable for the audience and are easy to understand

Topics + Recordings

  • Introduction to DSC x WiDS Datathon

  • Introduction to GitHub: Research Data Management and Reproducibility Librarian Vicky Rampin offers a crash course on GitHub/Git. Familiarize yourselves with one of the most commonly used platforms for storing, tracking, and collaborating on software projects.
  • Python 101: NYU Libraries guide for learning Python with several learning resources including access to LinkedIn Learning videos and web resources! (Note, if you are having difficulties accessing any of the links, please use an NYU account.)
  • R 101: NYU Libraries guide for learning R with several learning resources including access to LinkedIn Learning videos and web resources! (Note, if you are having difficulties accessing any of the links, please use an NYU account.)

  • Data Analysis: Graduate Student Spriha Jha walks us through several basic data analysis techniques to implement within your very own data science projects! This is a great way to get your feet wet into the world of data science!
  • Hypothesis Testing: Professor Pascal Wallisch talks about building a hypothesis and testing your hypothesis with different methods.

  • Introduction to ML Workshop: Graduate Student Adeet Patel will walk you through the basic concepts of ML and how to apply it within your own projects!
  • Introduction to Machine Learning: An introduction to machine learning by Professor He He! Lecture 1b offers some basic insight into understanding what machine learning is and how you can apply it to specific problems.
  • Introduction to Clustering: This lecture by Professor Pascal Wallisch covers basic clustering methods that can be used for customer segmentation.
